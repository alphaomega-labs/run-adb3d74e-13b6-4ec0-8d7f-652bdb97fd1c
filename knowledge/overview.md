# Literature Overview

Contract-governed multi-agent research orchestration is currently supported by a broad but uneven literature base. The available corpus shows strong practical convergence on graph-like workflow decomposition, role specialization, and validator-mediated execution control, but it is still weak on theorem-grade guarantees for long-horizon soundness. This distillation therefore separates what is established, what is contested, and what remains an open contribution space for a Quarks-style formalization.

The strongest empirical agreement concerns architecture. Across PaperOrchestra (arxiv:2604.05018), SagaLLM (url:https://doi.org/10.14778/3750601.3750611), AI Scientist-v2 (url:https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2504.08066), and a broad survey layer (url:https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2504.19678; url:https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2508.14111), workflows are decomposed into role-specialized units with explicit handoff interfaces. Even when the domain changes (writing, network operations, scheduling, clinical decision support), the same pattern appears: a planning authority proposes subgoals, workers execute under local context, and a critic/validator stage accepts, rejects, or reroutes outputs. This cross-domain recurrence suggests a reusable definition seed for Quarks: an orchestration graph G=(V,E) where nodes are typed phase contracts and edges are guarded transitions driven by validator predicates.

A second agreement concerns the need for runtime governance, but sources differ in how formal that governance is. Prompt-native semantic runtime work (url:https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19059674) emphasizes inference-time policy checks and provenance-bearing traces. Security-focused work (url:https://doi.org/10.1016/j.icte.2025.12.001; url:https://doi.org/10.3390/info16050365; url:https://doi.org/10.1109/eiecc67963.2025.11409558) emphasizes attack surfaces and trust scoring at agent boundaries. SAMF (url:https://doi.org/10.20944/preprints202604.1025.v1) pushes further toward validation-first progression semantics, and DeViLL (url:https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-98660-4_12) provides a formal-method bridge where toolchain interactions are explicitly orchestrated against methodological constraints. The consensus is that unvalidated free-form transitions are brittle; the unresolved issue is whether current policy checks can be lifted into proof-carrying transition rules.

The corpus also contributes notation and equation seeds, although sparsely and unevenly. PaperOrchestra provides an explicit pipeline map P=(P_tex,P_pdf)=W(I,E,T,G,F), with the special case F=empty as an auto-generation regime (arxiv:2604.05018). SymbolicAI (arxiv:2402.00854) contributes a formal language perspective, including sequence-space symbols S and expression/operator maps over symbolic structures. Agentic science survey content (url:https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2508.14111) introduces a human-scientist-to-model abstraction with a selection/training objective form. These seeds are not yet a unified calculus, but they are sufficient to define a downstream notation bank: typed objects, validator predicates, transition relation, policy operator, trace object, and uncertainty-triggered reroute operator.

For Quarks, the most important assumption pattern is the typed-interface assumption: outputs can be normalized into contract-compatible structures and can be judged by validators before state transition. This appears explicitly in SAMF and in multiple orchestration systems that rely on schema-like handoffs or stage-specific acceptance checks (url:https://doi.org/10.20944/preprints202604.1025.v1; arxiv:2604.05018; url:https://doi.org/10.14778/3750601.3750611). A second recurrent assumption is recoverability: when a stage fails, the system can reroute, rollback, or branch without irrecoverably corrupting global state. This assumption is often implied rather than proved. A third assumption concerns provenance persistence: governance and auditability claims require trace continuity across role handoffs (url:https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19059674; url:https://doi.org/10.14778/3750601.3750611).

The contradiction structure in this corpus is less about direct factual disagreement and more about mismatched evidentiary standards. Engineering papers often report strong utility gains from orchestration but do not provide theorem-level guarantees on liveness, safety, or compositional soundness under repeated reroutes. Formal-method adjacent works provide stronger semantic discipline but limited adaptation to modern LLM stochasticity and uncertainty-calibrated branching. This creates a methodological contradiction: practical systems assume contracts and validators are sufficient for reliability, while formal traditions require explicit proof obligations for transition soundness, refinement, and failure containment. In Quarks terms, this is blocking for theorem claims but extendable for architectural claims.

A second contradiction concerns security and trust semantics. Security literature argues prompt-level defenses are insufficient because protocol and composition layers create additional exploit channels (url:https://doi.org/10.1016/j.icte.2025.12.001). Yet many orchestration papers treat validation as mostly functional quality control, not adversarial robustness. The result is a gap between correctness-under-benign-input assumptions and robustness-under-adversarial-interaction requirements. For a contract-governed graph, this means validator predicates must be modeled not only as quality checks but as adversarially aware guards with explicit failure regions.

A third contradiction concerns optimization objective versus governance objective. Agentic science systems such as Physics_GPT and ChemGraph show domain gains by prioritizing discovery throughput and iterative refinement (url:https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19130521; url:https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19305235; url:https://doi.org/10.1038/s42004-025-01776-9). Governance-oriented sources prioritize provenance, controllability, and policy compliance (url:https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19059674; url:https://doi.org/10.20944/preprints202604.1025.v1). The literature does not yet establish a principled theorem linking these objective families, especially under long-horizon branching. This is extendable, but it requires explicit tradeoff modeling in later phases.

A fourth contradiction appears in evaluation regimes. Several surveys and roadmaps report that many systems are tested on narrow or short-horizon tasks and that orchestration evaluations are not standardized (url:https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2501.11354; url:https://doi.org/10.1609/aaai.v39i27.35084; url:https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2504.19678). Claims about robust long-horizon autonomy therefore often rely on limited evidence. For the Quarks paper direction, this implies theorem statements must include boundary conditions and counterexample regimes, with explicit claims about what is proved versus what is only empirically suggested.

The taxonomy that emerges has six useful strata. First is orchestration core architecture: graph decomposition, role assignment, and stateful context management. Second is contract and verification semantics: validation gates, transition guards, and toolchain consistency constraints. Third is governance and security: provenance integrity, trust evaluation, and adversarial boundary conditions. Fourth is domain transfer evidence: chemistry, physics, clinical, and operations use cases that stress generality and reproducibility. Fifth is symbolic-neural control: hybrid representations where symbolic constraints stabilize stochastic generation. Sixth is survey/roadmap synthesis that exposes open gaps in evaluation and formal guarantees.

Consensus boundaries are clearer than novelty boundaries. Established facts include: role specialization improves manageability in complex pipelines; validator-gated transitions reduce uncontrolled failure propagation; and provenance-aware runtime controls are increasingly treated as mandatory for auditable systems. Contested facts include: whether current validation protocols are sufficient for long-horizon correctness; whether reroute/backtrack policies preserve global invariants under stochastic outputs; and whether transaction-like semantics in LLM workflows can be elevated from implementation heuristics to theorem-backed guarantees. The open novelty space is therefore defensible: define a contract-bounded transition system with typed interfaces, validator composition rules, and meta-orchestration operators; prove soundness or bounded-failure properties under explicit assumptions; and map counterexamples where assumptions fail.

Bridge opportunities exist but must be treated as opportunities, not settled equivalences. The strongest bridge candidate links formal-method interface contracts (DeViLL-like methodology) and transaction semantics (SagaLLM) with modern LLM orchestration validators. Another bridge links symbolic language constraints (SymbolicAI) with contract-typing over agent outputs, enabling proofs about admissible transition space rather than unconstrained text trajectories. A third bridge links trust-evaluation frameworks to guard-strengthening lemmas, where trust score thresholds parameterize transition eligibility. Each bridge is plausible, but each needs downstream derivation and proof obligations before it can be claimed as a contribution.

For problem setting design, the literature supports a concrete regime. Let a workflow be a directed graph G=(V,E). Each node v in V has an input type I_v, output type O_v, and local agent role r_v in {orchestrator, executor, scientist}. Each edge e=(u,v) has a contract C_e and validator family Phi_e. A transition u->v is enabled only if output y_u is type-compatible with I_v and satisfies Phi_e(y_u, m_t, pi_t), where m_t is persistent memory state and pi_t is the active policy state. Meta-orchestration applies an action a_t in {continue, reroute, backtrack, branch} based on validator outcomes and uncertainty signals. This seed captures the core architecture while leaving room for theorem refinement.

Failure regions should be explicit from the start. First, specification drift: validators pass locally but accumulated semantic drift breaks downstream assumptions. Second, adversarial contamination: protocol-level manipulations bypass prompt-level filters. Third, memory incoherence: provenance links degrade across reroutes, making audit claims invalid. Fourth, branching explosion: uncertainty-driven branching improves search coverage but destroys tractability or proof assumptions. Fifth, trust miscalibration: confidence or trust proxies are treated as guarantees without formal calibration bounds. These failure regions are consistent with limitations and warning signals in the acquired sources.

Methodological gaps are therefore concrete. The field lacks a commonly accepted theorem suite for orchestration graphs under stochastic agents. It lacks a standard contradiction-handling framework where unresolved conflicts are first-class objects tied to downstream resolution phases. It lacks notation-level interoperability between engineering orchestration papers and formal verification papers. And it lacks reproducible acceptance checks that separate architecture validity, security robustness, and scientific-claim grounding.

For downstream phases, this distillation implies three priorities. In literature review and hypothesis phases, formalize definitions, assumptions, lemma templates, and corollary boundaries with explicit source lineage. In derive-math methodology, prove at least transition soundness under bounded assumptions and provide counterexamples for assumption violations. In experiment-design and validation phases, use implementation-aligned traces not generic benchmark scores: check contract satisfaction rates, reroute correctness traces, provenance preservation, and contradiction closure logs. This keeps the paper anchored to both mathematical rigor and the actual Quarks orchestration substrate.

In summary, the collected corpus strongly supports the architectural premise of contract-governed multi-agent orchestration and strongly motivates validator-centric control, but it does not yet settle the formal theory for long-horizon correctness under uncertainty. That unresolved interval is exactly where a Quarks contribution can be both novel and defensible: establish a typed transition calculus, make guard and policy assumptions explicit, prove what can be proved, and expose counterexample boundaries where guarantees stop.
